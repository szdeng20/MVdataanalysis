Conceptual Foundation

Coagulation & Flocculation Theory

Suspended particles in raw water carry a negative surface charge, keeping them dispersed by electrostatic repulsion (zeta potential). Coagulation neutralises this charge; flocculation then bridges particles into settleable floc.

⚡

Why Particles Stay Suspended

Colloidal particles (0.001–1 µm) develop a negative surface charge due to isomorphous substitution and ionisation. This creates an electrical double layer (Stern + diffuse layer) with a repulsive energy barrier. Zeta potential (typically −20 to −30 mV) keeps particles stable.

⚗️

Coagulation Mechanisms

Four mechanisms act simultaneously:

  • Double-layer compression — counter-ions compress the diffuse layer
  • Charge neutralisation — Al³⁺/Fe³⁺ species adsorb on particle surface
  • Sweep flocculation — Al(OH)₃ precipitate enmeshes particles
  • Adsorption & bridging — polymer bridges link particles
🔄

Role of pH

Alum hydrolysis produces Al(OH)₃ only within pH 6–8. Outside this range, soluble Al³⁺ or aluminate Al(OH)₄⁻ forms instead — neither coagulates effectively. pH also affects particle surface charge and coagulant speciation. Optimal pH: 6.5–7.5 for alum.

🧪

Role of Alkalinity

Each mg/L of alum consumes ~0.45 mg/L of alkalinity to produce Al(OH)₃. If alkalinity is too low (<40 mg/L CaCO₃), pH drops sharply during coagulation, moving outside the optimal zone. High alkalinity buffers against this pH crash. Lime or soda ash may be dosed to boost alkalinity.

Al₂(SO₄)₃ + 6HCO₃⁻ → 2Al(OH)₃↓ + 3SO₄²⁻ + 6CO₂

🧱

Role of Hardness

Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ (hardness ions) can compress the double layer and assist coagulation at low doses. However, very hard water (>300 mg/L CaCO₃) may compete with Al³⁺ for floc surface sites, reduce coagulant effectiveness, and increase sludge volume. Hardness ions remain in solution as TDS unless specifically removed.

📐

Flocculation Kinetics

Floc growth follows: perikinetic (Brownian motion, sub-µm) and orthokinetic (velocity gradients, >1 µm) phases. The velocity gradient G (s⁻¹) and detention time t must balance: G·t = 10⁴ to 10⁵. Too high G breaks floc; too low G fails to aggregate it. Gentle mixing (G = 10–75 s⁻¹) for 20–40 min is typical.

Water Quality Parameters

Total Suspended Solids vs Total Dissolved Solids

TSS and TDS describe physically different populations of material in water. Understanding the distinction is fundamental to selecting the right treatment process and monitoring treated water quality.

◉ TSS — Total Suspended Solids

Particles retained on a 0.45 µm filter. Visible turbidity. Can be removed by sedimentation, filtration, or coagulation/flocculation. TSS is what coagulation primarily targets.

Key Relationship

Where Hardness Fits: TSS or TDS?

💧

Hardness is a TDS Contributor

Hardness ions — calcium (Ca²⁺) and magnesium (Mg²⁺) — are fully dissolved in water and therefore classified as TDS, not TSS. They pass freely through a 0.45 µm filter and cannot be seen or removed by coagulation or filtration alone.

However, when Ca²⁺ precipitates as CaCO₃ (e.g. during lime softening or at high pH), it becomes a suspended solid (TSS) that can then be settled and filtered out. This is the basis of lime softening.

⚠️ Coagulation reduces TSS (turbidity) — it does NOT significantly reduce TDS or hardness.

Water Chemistry

Water Hardness: Causes, Types & Removal

Hardness is caused by dissolved divalent metal ions, primarily Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺. It causes scale in pipes and boilers, interferes with soap lathering, and in treatment plants affects coagulation chemistry and sludge production.

Types of Hardness

✓ Temporary (Carbonate) Hardness

Caused by Ca(HCO₃)₂ and Mg(HCO₃)₂ dissolved from limestone. Associated with bicarbonate alkalinity. Can be removed by boiling — CO₂ driven off, CaCO₃ precipitates.

Ca(HCO₃)₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O + CO₂↑

Removal: boiling, lime addition, lime-soda softening.

✗ Permanent (Non-Carbonate) Hardness

Caused by CaSO₄, CaCl₂, MgSO₄, MgCl₂. NOT associated with bicarbonate. Cannot be removed by boiling. Remains dissolved regardless of temperature.

CaSO₄ → Ca²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ (stays dissolved)

Removal: lime-soda ash process, ion exchange, RO, nanofiltration.

Treatment Methods

Hardness Removal Processes

Method 01

Lime-Soda Ash Softening

Lime (Ca(OH)₂) raises pH to ~10–11, precipitating CaCO₃ and Mg(OH)₂ as settleable solids. Soda ash (Na₂CO₃) removes non-carbonate Ca²⁺. Most common large-scale process.

Ca²⁺ + CO₃²⁻ → CaCO₃↓ | Mg²⁺ + 2OH⁻ → Mg(OH)₂↓
Method 02

Ion Exchange (Zeolite/Resin)

Cation exchange resin replaces Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ with Na⁺. Common in domestic water softeners. Does NOT reduce TDS — replaces hardness ions with Na⁺. Regenerated with NaCl brine.

Ca²⁺ + 2Na-Resin → Ca-Resin₂ + 2Na⁺
Method 03

Reverse Osmosis (RO)

Pressure-driven membrane process. Rejects >95% of dissolved ions including Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺. Reduces both TDS and hardness simultaneously. Used in desalination and industrial boiler feed water treatment.

Semi-permeable membrane: pore size ~0.0001 µm rejects ions
Method 04

Nanofiltration (NF)

Removes divalent ions (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, SO₄²⁻) selectively while passing monovalent ions (Na⁺, Cl⁻). Lower pressure than RO. Often used for selective hardness removal while retaining some dissolved minerals.

~90–98% rejection of Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺; ~20–40% rejection of Na⁺
Method 05

Electrodialysis (ED)

Ion-selective membranes with an electric field drive cations and anions apart. Effective for brackish water demineralisation. Less common for hardness alone but reduces overall TDS.

Cation-exchange + anion-exchange membranes in stack configuration
Method 06

Distillation / Boiling

Vaporises water, leaving dissolved solids behind. Removes temporary hardness effectively. Impractical at large scale due to energy cost. Used for laboratory-grade deionised water.

Only removes temporary hardness (carbonate forms) completely
Connecting the Concepts

Relationship: Hardness ↔ TSS ↔ TDS ↔ Coagulation

Hardness → TDS (not TSS)

In its dissolved form, hardness contributes to TDS. Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ pass through 0.45 µm filters. Coagulation does NOT remove hardness — it removes TSS (turbidity/suspended particles).

Lime Softening Converts Hardness to TSS

Adding lime raises pH until CaCO₃ and Mg(OH)₂ precipitate. These precipitates are now TSS — they can be settled and filtered. Hardness has been converted from TDS → TSS → removed.

Coagulation + Softening Together

Many plants run lime softening before or alongside coagulation. Lime softening raises pH and removes hardness. Then CO₂ or acid re-stabilises pH, and coagulant removes remaining TSS. This treats both hardness (TDS) and turbidity (TSS).

Effect of Hardness on Coagulation

Moderate hardness (50–200 mg/L) slightly aids coagulation by compressing the electrical double layer (Ca²⁺ acts like a secondary coagulant aid). Very high hardness (>300 mg/L) may reduce coagulant performance and significantly increases sludge mass.
